Understanding Every Layer Under the Hood

Chapter 1

Atoms: The Foundation of Matter

Everything in the physical universe is composed of matter, and the fundamental building block of matter is the atom. To understand how a computer works, we must zoom in to the atomic scale, where the physical laws that govern electricity begin.

The Structure of the Atom

At the center of every atom lies the nucleus, which is extremely dense and contains two types of subatomic particles:

  • Protons: Particles that carry a positive (+) electric charge.
  • Neutrons: Particles that carry no charge (neutral).

Orbiting the nucleus is a cloud of much lighter particles:

  • Electrons: Particles that carry a negative (-) electric charge.
Figure 1.1: Bohr model of an atom, featuring a central nucleus of protons and neutrons orbited by negative electrons.

The number of protons in an atom's nucleus defines its atomic number and determines which chemical element it is (for example, Hydrogen has 1 proton, Carbon has 6, and Silicon has 14). In a neutral, stable atom, the number of electrons orbiting the nucleus exactly equals the number of protons in the nucleus, making the overall net electrical charge of the atom zero.

Chapter 2

Electrons and Valence Shells

Electrons do not orbit the nucleus in random paths. Instead, they occupy specific energy levels called electron shells.

  • Each shell can hold only a specific maximum number of electrons.
  • The shells are filled from the innermost to the outermost.
  • The first shell holds a maximum of 2 electrons.
  • The second shell holds a maximum of 8 electrons.
  • The third shell holds a maximum of 18 electrons (though it is stable with 8).
Figure 1.2: Electron shells of a Silicon atom. The 4 valence electrons in the outermost shell are key to semiconductor conduction.

Valence Electrons

The outermost electron shell of an atom is called its valence shell, and the electrons residing in this shell are called valence electrons.

Valence electrons require the least amount of external energy to be stripped away from the atom, and they are the only electrons that participate in chemical bonding and electrical conduction.

Chapter 3

Electricity: The Flow of Charge

Electricity is the physical phenomenon associated with the presence and motion of electric charge. Specifically, the controlled movement of charge through a medium.

If we apply an external force—such as an electric field—to a material, we can push valence electrons out of their orbits. Once free, these electrons drift through the material, jumping from the valence shell of one atom to the valence shell of the next. This continuous, directed drift of free electrons through a conductor is what we call electricity.

Chapter 4

Voltage: The Electrical Pressure

Voltage (V), also called electric potential difference, is the difference in electric potential energy per unit charge between two points in space. Think of voltage as electrical pressure.

Mathematical Definition of Voltage
V = WQ
Where:
V = Voltage in Volts (V)
W = Work done (energy transferred) in Joules (J)
Q = Electric charge in Coulombs (C)
Chapter 5

Current: The Rate of Flow

Electric current (I) is the rate at which electric charge flows past a specific point in a circuit. It is measured in Amperes (A). One Ampere represents one Coulomb of charge passing through a cross-section of a conductor in one second (I = Q/t).

Conventional Current vs. Electron Flow: Because electrons carry a negative charge, they physically flow from the negative (-) terminal of a voltage source to the positive (+) terminal. By historical convention, we draw current flowing from positive (+) to negative (-). Both representations work as long as usage is consistent.

Chapter 6

Resistance and Ohm's Law

Opposition to the flow of electric current is called Resistance (R). It represents the atomic collisions that slow down electrons, converting electrical energy into heat.

Ohm's Law
V = I × R   or   I = VR

If you increase the voltage (electrical pressure), current increases. If you increase resistance (obstruction), current decreases.

Chapter 7

Silicon: The Essential Element

Silicon (Si) is a semiconductor: a material with electrical conductivity between that of a conductor and an insulator. It has 4 valence electrons. In a solid crystal structure, silicon atoms share their valence electrons with four neighbors in stable covalent bonds, locking the electrons in place.

Chapter 8

Semiconductors and Doping

Doping is the process of adding impurities to a pure semiconductor to alter its conductivity:

  • N-Type (Negative): Silicon doped with a group V element (like Phosphorus), which has 5 valence electrons. This introduces an unbound free electron into the crystal lattice.
  • P-Type (Positive): Silicon doped with a group III element (like Boron), which has 3 valence electrons. This leaves a missing bond, creating a positive charge vacancy called a hole.

Chapter 9

MOSFETs: The Digital Switch

A MOSFET is an electronically controlled switch. An NMOS transistor consists of a P-type substrate and two N-type wells (Source and Drain). A Gate electrode sits above the region between source and drain, insulated by a thin layer of Silicon Dioxide (SiO2).

Chapter 10

CMOS: The Engine of Modern Logic

CMOS (Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) architecture pairs NMOS and PMOS transistors. In a CMOS inverter (NOT gate), a PMOS transistor pulls the output to VDD (logic 1) when the input is low, and an NMOS transistor pulls the output to ground (logic 0) when the input is high.

Chapter 11

Binary & Number Systems

In digital systems, voltage ranges represent two states: HIGH (1) and LOW (0). This base-2 system is called binary. A single binary digit is a bit. Eight bits grouped together form a byte (28 = 256 states).

To represent negative integers, modern computers use Two's Complement notation. In an N-bit system, the most significant bit carries a negative weight. For example, in a 4-bit system, 1011 represents -8 + 0 + 2 + 1 = -5. This notation has the elegant mathematical property that binary addition works identically for both positive and negative numbers.

Chapter 12

Boolean Algebra

Boolean algebra is a branch of algebra where the values of variables are the truth values true (1) and false (0). The three basic operators are:

  • AND (·): Output is 1 only if all inputs are 1.
  • OR (+): Output is 1 if at least one input is 1.
  • NOT (X): Outputs the opposite state of the input.

Using these three operators, any logical assertion or mathematical calculation can be expressed as a Boolean function. Boolean simplification laws (such as De Morgan's laws) allow circuit designers to reduce the number of transistors required to execute a function.

Chapter 13

Logic Gates

Logic gates are the hardware implementation of Boolean functions. By combining NMOS and PMOS transistors in specific configurations, we build the basic computational units of a computer. AND, OR, and NOT form the foundational logic blocks.

Figure 2.1: Schematic symbols for the standard AND, OR, and NOT (Inverter) logic gates.

By attaching an inverter (a NOT gate) to the output of other gates, we create NAND (NOT AND) and NOR (NOT OR) gates. These are particularly special because they are universal gates—meaning absolutely any logical circuit, up to and including a full processor, can be constructed using only NAND gates. This drastically simplifies mass semiconductor manufacturing.

We also have the XOR (Exclusive OR) gate, which outputs a 1 only if its inputs differ (one is 1, the other is 0). If both are 0 or both are 1, it outputs 0. XOR is the secret engine behind binary mathematical addition.

Figure 2.2: Advanced universal and exclusive logic gates derived from combinations of the basic gates. Notice the small inversion circles on the outputs.
Chapter 14

Logic Adders: Math in Hardware

How do we actually calculate using voltage? Let's look at basic binary addition. When adding two single bits (A and B), there are four possibilities:

  • 0 + 0 = 0 (Sum: 0, Carry: 0)
  • 0 + 1 = 1 (Sum: 1, Carry: 0)
  • 1 + 0 = 1 (Sum: 1, Carry: 0)
  • 1 + 1 = 10 (Sum: 0, Carry: 1)

Notice the pattern: The Sum bit is 1 only when the inputs differ. This is the exact logic of an XOR Gate! The Carry bit is 1 only when both inputs are 1. This is the exact logic of an AND Gate! By wiring an XOR and an AND gate in parallel, we create a Half Adder.

Figure 2.3: A Half Adder circuit using an XOR gate for the Sum and an AND gate for the Carry out.

But there's a problem: to add multi-digit binary numbers (like adding 8-bit numbers together), we have to carry over values to the next column. A Half Adder only accepts two inputs. It cannot accept a carry coming from a previous column.

By cascading two Half Adders together and placing an OR gate at the end to catch any overflow, we construct a Full Adder. A Full Adder accepts three inputs (A, B, and Carry-In) and produces a Sum and a Carry-Out.

If we daisy-chain 64 of these Full Adders in a row—connecting the Carry-Out of one into the Carry-In of the next—we have just built a 64-bit Ripple-Carry Adder. We have successfully turned physical transistors into an entity capable of instantly calculating massive mathematical equations.

Chapter 15

Multiplexers & Decoders

To control the routing of data in a processor, we use routing circuits:

  • Multiplexer (MUX): Acts as a selector switch. It takes multiple input signals, and routes a single selected input to the output based on control line inputs.
  • Decoder: Takes an N-bit binary value and activates exactly one of its 2N output lines. We use decoders to choose which hardware component or memory row is active.
Chapter 16

Latches: Feedback Loops

All memory is built from logic gates configured with feedback loops, where outputs wrap back to inputs. This feedback enables the circuit to lock, or "latch," into a stable state even after the input stimuli are removed.

The simplest static memory circuit is the SR Latch (Set-Reset), constructed from two cross-coupled NOR gates. Activating the Set line forces output Q to 1; activating the Reset line forces it to 0. When both inputs are 0, the feedback loop retains the state indefinitely, serving as a 1-bit memory.

Figure 3.1: Cross-coupled NOR gates forming an SR Latch capable of storing a single bit via feedback.
Chapter 17

Flip-Flops & Clock Signals

An SR Latch is a great start, but unsynchronized feedback memory is chaotic. If the inputs change at slightly different times due to physical wire lengths, the memory might latch the wrong value. This is called a timing hazard.

We resolve this by adding a master conductor: a Clock (CLK). A clock is an oscillator circuit that pulses high and low at a fixed frequency (e.g., a 4 GHz CPU clock pulses 4 billion times per second). We wire the latch so it only accepts new data when the clock ticks.

This upgraded circuit is called a D Flip-Flop. It is "edge-triggered"—meaning it samples the input data state D only at the exact microscopic instant the clock signal transitions from low to high (the rising edge). It locks that value to the output Q until the next clock cycle. This guarantees that data propagates predictably across the CPU stages in perfect synchronization.

Chapter 18

Registers: The Working Memory

A single Flip-Flop stores exactly one bit. But computers operate on large numbers like 64-bit integers. How do we store them?

By placing 64 D Flip-Flops side-by-side and wiring them to a single, shared Clock line, we construct a 64-bit Register. When the clock ticks, all 64 bits of data are captured and stored simultaneously. Registers are placed directly next to the calculation circuits (like the Adders) to hold the immediate working numbers being calculated.

Figure 3.2: A 64-bit register capturing data on the rising edge of a clock pulse. The slash denotes a multi-wire bus.

However, we can't build millions of gigabytes of memory using Registers—they require too many transistors (6 per bit) and take up too much physical space on the silicon die.

  • SRAM (Static RAM): Utilizes 6-transistor latch circuits (like registers) to store data. It is extremely fast and holds state as long as power is applied. It is used for small, high-speed CPU caches.
  • DRAM (Dynamic RAM): Stores each bit as an electrical charge inside a single microscopic capacitor, gated by a single transistor. Capacitors naturally leak energy, meaning DRAM must be continuously rewritten ("refreshed") thousands of times per second. DRAM is much cheaper and denser than SRAM, serving as your main computer memory (RAM).
Chapter 19

The Von Neumann Architecture

Virtually all modern general-purpose computers utilize the **Von Neumann Architecture**. In this layout, both the program's code instructions and data variables are stored in the same unified memory space (RAM).

Figure 4.1: Block diagram of the classical Von Neumann computer architecture.
Chapter 20

Inside the CPU: ALU & Registers

We've built Logic Adders to calculate, and Registers to store data. How do we make them "programmable"? Instead of physically re-wiring the circuits for every new math problem, we use Multiplexers (MUX) to electronically route the signals. By sending specific 1s and 0s to the MUX select pins, we can choose whether the adder circuit's inputs receive data from Register A or Register B, and where the result goes.

The CPU is constructed from these specific functional hardware components wired together with multiplexers:

  • ALU (Arithmetic Logic Unit): The computer's grand calculator. It is a massive block built from ripple-carry adders, subtractors, and logical gate arrays. It takes two input binary values, performs a specific operation (addition, bitwise AND, etc.), and outputs the result.
  • General Purpose Registers: A small bank of incredibly fast 64-bit storage circuits located right next to the ALU. These hold the immediate variables being calculated.
  • Program Counter (PC): A special register that stores the memory address of the next instruction waiting to be executed.
  • Instruction Register (IR): A register that holds the binary code of the instruction currently being executed.
  • Control Unit: The conductor of the orchestra. It decodes the instruction bits sitting inside the IR and triggers the coordinate control signals to the multiplexers, routing data across the internal buses.
Chapter 21

The Programmable Cycle: Fetch-Decode-Execute

This is the magical moment where static hardware becomes a dynamic, programmable machine capable of running software. The CPU operates by continuously executing a three-stage machine cycle, driven strictly by the rhythmic pulsing of the system clock:

  1. Fetch: The Control Unit reads the memory address currently stored in the Program Counter (PC). It fetches the binary instruction at that address from the main RAM, and loads it into the Instruction Register (IR). The PC is then automatically incremented by 1 so it points to the next instruction in line.
  2. Decode: The Control Unit decodes the opcode bits in the IR. If the instruction is 0010, the Control Unit might recognize this as the "ADD" command. It immediately sends voltage signals to the internal multiplexers to route the requested registers into the ALU's inputs, and configures the ALU to perform addition.
  3. Execute: The operation physically occurs as voltage propagates through the logic gates (e.g., the ALU performs the ripple-carry addition). On the next clock edge, the final calculated result is written back to a destination register.

This cycle repeats billions of times per second (e.g., 4 GHz). By feeding the CPU different lists of binary instructions in memory (a program), the exact same physical logic gates can be directed to calculate graphics for a video game, route network traffic, or run an operating system.

Chapter 22

ISA: The Interface Contract

The **Instruction Set Architecture (ISA)** is the abstract boundary separating CPU hardware from compiler software. It defines the supported assembly instructions, operand formats, and registers. There are two major paradigms:

  • x86-64: Complex Instruction Set Computer (CISC). Instructions vary in length (1 to 15 bytes), supporting complex instructions that load, add, and store memory in a single cycle. Standard in PCs and servers.
  • ARM64: Reduced Instruction Set Computer (RISC). Fixed-length instructions (4 bytes) operating only on registers. Memory access is limited to explicit Load/Store instructions. Dominated by efficiency, standard in smartphones and Apple Silicon.
Chapter 23

Instruction Pipelines & Branch Prediction

To increase execution speed, modern CPUs overlap instruction execution stages using an **instruction pipeline**, resembling a factory assembly line. While one instruction is executing, the next is decoding, and a third is being fetched.

Figure 5.1: Pipeline execution timeline showing overlapped processing stages.

Pipelines suffer from **hazards** when instruction paths split (conditional branches). CPUs use hardware **Branch Prediction** blocks to guess the direction of execution pathways. If the guess is correct, execution continues instantly; if wrong, the speculative instructions are flushed, incurring a penalty.

Chapter 24

Out-of-Order Execution & Multi-Core

Modern performance demands go beyond pipelining:

  • Out-of-Order Execution (OoO): Rather than stalling on slow memory fetches, the CPU schedules and executes instructions out of sequence whenever their input dependencies are resolved, reorganizing results into sequence before final commit.
  • SIMD (Single Instruction Multiple Data): Vector execution blocks that execute one instruction (e.g. ADD) across arrays of values in parallel.
  • Multi-core: Packaging multiple complete CPU cores on a single silicon die. Cores operate in parallel, using hardware **Cache Coherency** protocols to sync data edits in local caches.
Chapter 25

The Compilation Pipeline

High-level code (like C or Rust) must be translated into raw binary machine instructions. The toolchain pipeline includes:

Figure 6.1: Visual workflow of the compilation toolchain converting code to machine files.

The compiler translates structure into assembly text. The assembler encodes assembly commands into a binary **Object File**. The **Linker** resolves symbol dependencies across multiple object files and library references, outputting a unified executable file.

Chapter 26

Executables & Memory Layout

An executable file (such as Windows PE or Unix ELF) groups machine instructions and variables into structured segments. When launched, the OS maps these segments into virtual memory regions:

  • .text segment: Contains the binary machine code instructions (configured as read-only).
  • .data & .bss: Store global and static variables.
  • Stack: Fast, local memory managed automatically by the CPU to handle function frames, local variables, and return jump vectors (grows downward).
  • Heap: Dynamically allocated memory managed at runtime via allocation requests like `malloc` (grows upward).
Chapter 27

Processes & Thread Scheduling

Operating Systems manage concurrency, allocating system hardware to application processes:

  • Process: An isolated sandbox representing a running program. It owns its unique virtual address map, files, and threading contexts.
  • Thread: The unit of CPU execution inside a process. Multiple threads share their parent process's memory space, but possess local stack states.
  • Context Switching: The process where the OS scheduler triggers timer interrupts to pause a running thread, write its active register set to memory, load a different thread's register set, and resume execution.
Chapter 28

Virtual Memory & Address Translation

To isolate programs from each other, OS kernels map physical memory using **Virtual Memory** systems. Processes reference virtual addresses, which are translated into physical addresses by the hardware.

Figure 7.1: Virtual-to-physical address translation mapping page blocks.

Memory is split into **Pages** (e.g. 4KB blocks) mapped to **Frames** in RAM via a **Page Table**. The hardware **Memory Management Unit (MMU)** intercepts address requests to perform translation. A hardware cache called the **Translation Lookaside Buffer (TLB)** accelerates this process.

Chapter 29

System Calls & Privilege Rings

To protect hardware integrity, CPUs support execution rings:

  • Ring 0 (Kernel Mode): Complete hardware instruction privileges. The OS kernel resides here.
  • Ring 3 (User Mode): Restrictive execution. User programs reside here.

When user programs require hardware actions (e.g., writing a file or allocating socket resources), they must execute a **System Call** (syscall). This triggers a software interrupt (trap instruction), switching the CPU to Ring 0 and jumping to secure kernel routines before returning to Ring 3.

Chapter 30

The Application Event Loop

GUI applications must remain active to process user events. They run a continuous loop called the **Event Loop**:

while (GetMessage(&msg)) {
    TranslateMessage(&msg);
    DispatchMessage(&msg);
}

When input hardware (a key or mouse movement) triggers interrupts, the OS kernel adds the event to the active application's queue. The application's event loop fetches, translates, and dispatches these messages to event handlers, updating the GUI state and triggering redraws.

Chapter 31

Rendering & GPU Acceleration

Rendering is the process of translating layout structures into screen pixels. To prevent flickering, applications use **Double Buffering**—drawing onto an off-screen Back Buffer and swapping it with the active Front Buffer during vertical synchronization (VSync).

Modern applications offload rasterization to the **GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)**. Unlike CPUs, GPUs contain thousands of simple arithmetic cores. By feeding geometric coordinates and **Shader programs** directly to the GPU, graphics engines can calculate pixel values in parallel, rendering complex frames in fractions of a millisecond.

Chapter 32

Networking & Socket Communications

Applications transmit data using the **TCP/IP Protocol Stack**:

The Application Layer generates the message; the Transport Layer (TCP) packages it into segments and manages reliability; the Network Layer (IP) addresses segments into packets and handles routing; and the Link Layer translates packets into physical ethernet or Wi-Fi waveforms.
